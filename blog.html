Valentina (deleteimage): Cyanotypes, Glass Cubes & the Quiet Work of Making

The multidisciplinary artist talks light, process, and why slowing down has become its own kind of art.

By Spencer Dewbury

First off — what do you want the internet to know you as? You're a woman of many names.

The internet can know me as Valentina, or deleteimage, which is my Instagram handle where my art lives.

What were your early days on the internet like? Did you start your art journey before you started using the internet, or after?

I grew up very online due to being generally isolated when I was younger. I used the internet as a creative escape, so I started with photography and image manipulation on free applications. The internet has always been an integral part of who I am, as someone introverted and expressive. When I moved out and went to college, I started experimenting more with traditional art and 3D mediums.

I like the cyanotypes done on the fabric and the various tree barks — it's insanely cool. And your bichromate prints. They could all serve as album art and stand-alone art pieces, obviously. Have you had musicians approach you to use your art?

I've had a few small and local musicians approach me to use my art. I really enjoy collaborating and finding that people resonate with my work and allow that to represent aspects of their work. I am a photographer at heart, so composition is one of my top priorities. I always imagine how my art would look on a web page, icon, or cover, so I do find success in musicians reaching out to me.

Bichromate prints — various subjects

Talk to me about the cubes.

I made a series of cyanotypes on prisms — a few being cubes and another being a rectangular prism. To be honest, I didn't even know if my project would work; even a few people in my life thought I was being too ambitious. I've experimented with cyanotypes on wood, fabric, and glass, but I wanted to layer my images in real life. I wanted to do something I haven't seen with cyanotype before, so I ordered glass cubes. It was a lot of trial and error and long days.

I made a few cyanotypes on prisms where there's an image on the two opposite faces, and another cube where there's an image on four faces — not including the top or bottom. Before I started, I used Blender, a 3D modelling application, to see what images paired well with each other when layered. I cyanotyped images of branches and shadows being layered onto sides of buildings, then applied them to the cube — and it created its own inner world. Because of the light refracting, the perspective changes based on where you're looking. There's something really special about taking two images from two different locations and having them mesh together to create their own place.

Is there an art form you want to take on that you haven't yet?

I want to try metalworking because I'm interested in pushing my photographic ideas into a sculptural medium.

Cyanotype series

How do you plan on expanding your art and your career — both in the present and future? What's next?

I'm still completing my BFA, so right now my focus is on experimentation and skill building. I tend to work very intensely and sometimes burn out, so lately I've been slowing down and creating more intentionally. Long term, I want to continue my education and explore new ways of presenting my inner thoughts in a physical space.

Where can people buy your art? Are you doing a showcase anytime soon?

Right now, I don't have a system for people to buy my art. Most of the time, I do not sell the art I showcase online because it is conceptual and meant to be experienced visually. I have done local vending with prints and smaller-scale art projects. With the pottery I am making in the upcoming months, I plan on selling stuff that can be reached out to me by direct message or email.

I know you've done flea markets and the like in the past. Anything for us to look forward to in the future?

Lots of ceramics! Clay has been grounding for me, and I'm excited to keep making functional pieces that carry a peaceful yet strong presence.

Mixed media diptych

On a personal note — how was your year?

2025 has definitely been a hard year for me emotionally. As I'm getting older and nearly graduating from college, I am starting to settle into the fact that I am going to actually live my career as an artist. Personally, I have felt a shift in my work as I'm focused on making more intentional and well thought out art. I have definitely burnt myself out, but in 2026, I plan on prioritizing my mental health and stress levels.
